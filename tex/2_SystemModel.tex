\chapter{System Model}\label{ch:SystemModel}
\section{Introduction}
The design of a high-performance control system for a three-phase Active Voltage Source Rectifier (VSR) necessitates
a precise mathematical representation of its physical dynamics. While the physical topology of the VSR—consisting of
boost inductors, active switches (IGBTs), and a DC-link capacitor—is well established, its behavior under non-ideal
grid conditions presents complex modeling challenges.\par
Under balanced grid conditions, the system can be effectively modeled and controlled using a single synchronous Reference
Frame ($dq$). However, practical power systems frequently experience voltage unbalances due to faults, asymmetrical loads,
or transmission line unevenness. In such scenarios, the standard $dq$ model becomes insufficient because the negative-sequence
voltage components appear as oscillating terms at twice the grid frequency ($2\omega$) in the synchronous frame. These
oscillations propagate through the system, causing double-frequency ripples in the DC-link voltage and distorting the
input currents.\par
Therefore, to accurately capture the system's behavior under unbalanced conditions, it is necessary to move beyond
simple time-domain equations. This chapter develops a comprehensive mathematical model of the VSR by employing 
Symmetrical Component Theory. This approach decomposes the coupled three-phase system into decoupled positive
and negative sequence networks. Furthermore, by applying Clarke ($\alpha\beta$) and Park ($dq$) transformations
to both sequences, the time-varying AC quantities are converted into constant DC quantities, which are essential
for the design of linear PI controllers and the predictive model required for Model Predictive Control (MPC).\par
\section{Mathematical model of VSR}
The voltage equations for the 3-phase system shown in figure[\ref{fig:topology}] can be written as:
\begin{equation}
    \begin{gathered}
        e_{a} = L \frac{di_a}{dt} + R i_a + \mathcal{V}_a\\
        e_{b} = L \frac{di_b}{dt} + R i_b + \mathcal{V}_b\\
        e_{c} = L \frac{di_c}{dt} + R i_c + \mathcal{V}_c
    \end{gathered}
    \label{eq:voltage_eq}
\end{equation}
where $e_{a}, e_{b}, e_{c}$ are the grid phase voltages, $i_a, i_b, i_c$ are the phase currents, $L$ is the input inductance,
$R$ is the input resistance, $\mathcal{V}_a, \mathcal{V}_b, \mathcal{V}_c$ are the rectifier terminal phase voltages.\par
The three phase voltages and currents can be represented as follows:
\begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
        e_a &= E_{pk} \cdot \cos(\omega t) \\
        e_b &= E_{pk} \cdot \cos(\omega t - \frac{2\pi}{3}) \\
        e_c &= E_{pk} \cdot \cos(\omega t - \frac{4\pi}{3}) \\
        i_a &= I_{pk} \cdot \cos(\omega t + \phi_a) \\
        i_b &= I_{pk} \cdot \cos(\omega t - \frac{2\pi}{3} + \phi_b) \\
        i_c &= I_{pk} \cdot \cos(\omega t - \frac{4\pi}{3} + \phi_c)
    \end{split}
\end{equation}
The terminal voltages are determined by the modulation strategy, the DC link voltage and common mode voltage.\par
\begin{equation}
    \begin{gathered}
        \mathcal{V}_a = S_a V_{dc} + V_{cm}\\
        \mathcal{V}_b = S_b V_{dc} + V_{cm}\\
        \mathcal{V}_c = S_c V_{dc} + V_{cm}
    \end{gathered}
    \label{eq:common_mode_voltage}
\end{equation}
where $S_a, S_b, S_c$ are the switching states of the converter switches, $V_{dc}$ is the DC link voltage and
$V_{cm}$ is the common mode voltage.\par
The common mode voltage can be calculated by adding the three phase voltages in equation[\ref{eq:voltage_eq}]
and the current equations for the three-phase system, $i_a + i_b + i_c = 0$, which leads to the following
equation:
\begin{equation}
    \begin{gathered}
        V_{cm} =\frac{1}{3}(e_a + e_b + e_c) -\frac{1}{3}(S_a + S_b + S_c) V_{dc}\\
    \end{gathered}
    \label{eq:common_mode_voltage_calc}
\end{equation}
\section{Symmetrical sequence decomposition}
Incase of unbalanced grid conditions, the symmetrical sequence decomposition is used to analyze
the system as shown in figure[\ref{fig:symmetricalDecomposition}]. The three-phase parameters can
be decomposed into positive, negative, and zero sequence components represented by equation[\ref{eq:symmetrical_component}].
The positive sequence components represent a balanced set of phasors that rotate
in the same direction as the original phasors, the negative sequence components
rotate in the opposite direction, and the zero sequence components are in phase
with each other.\par
Since the neutral point of the system is not connected to the ground, the zero sequence component is not present in the system.
The derivation and Implementation of the symmetrical sequence decomposition is given in appendix [\ref{App:SymmetricalComponents}].
\begin{equation}
    \begin{gathered}
        X_{abc} = X^{p}_{abc} + X^{n}_{abc} + X^{0}_{abc}
    \end{gathered}
    \label{eq:symmetrical_component}
\end{equation}
where $X_{abc}$ is the three-phase parameter, $X^{p}_{abc}, X^{n}_{abc}, X^{0}_{abc}$ are the positive, negative,
and zero sequence components respectively.\par
Thus the space vector of the unbalanced system can be transformed using the Clarke transformation as given in appendix [\ref{App:ClarkeParkTransformation}]
to $\alpha\beta$ coordinates as space vector sum of the positive $X_{\alpha\beta}$ and negative $X_{\alpha\beta}$ sequence components.
The model of the system in equation[\ref{eq:alpha_beta_model}] can be expressed in $\alpha\beta$ coordinates as follows:\par
\begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
        e^{p}_{\alpha} &=  L  \frac{d}{dt} i^{p}_{\alpha} + R i^{p}_{\alpha} + \mathcal{V}^{p}_{\alpha} \\
        e^{p}_{\beta} &=  L  \frac{d}{dt} i^{p}_{\beta} + R i^{p}_{\beta} + \mathcal{V}^{p}_{\beta} \\
        e^{n}_{\alpha} &=  L  \frac{d}{dt} i^{n}_{\alpha} + R i^{n}_{\alpha} + \mathcal{V}^{n}_{\alpha} \\
        e^{n}_{\beta} &=  L  \frac{d}{dt} i^{n}_{\beta} + R i^{n}_{\beta} + \mathcal{V}^{n}_{\beta}
    \end{split}
    \label{eq:alpha_beta_model}
\end{equation}
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[scale=0.5]{img/symmetricalDecomposition.png}
    \caption{Phasor representation of unbalanced three-phase system}
    \label{fig:symmetricalDecomposition}
\end{figure}
The mathematical model of the system can be expressed in state space form in $\alpha\beta$ coordinates as follows
\begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
        \frac{d}{dt}
        \begin{bmatrix}
            i^{p}_{\alpha} \\
            i^{p}_{\beta}
        \end{bmatrix}
        =
        \begin{bmatrix}
            -\frac{R}{L} & 0            \\
            0            & -\frac{R}{L}
        \end{bmatrix}
        \begin{bmatrix}
            i^{p}_{\alpha} \\
            i^{p}_{\beta}
        \end{bmatrix}
        +
        \begin{bmatrix}
            \frac{1}{L} & 0           \\
            0           & \frac{1}{L}
        \end{bmatrix}
        \begin{bmatrix}
            e^{p}_{\alpha} \\
            e^{p}_{\beta}
        \end{bmatrix}
        +
        \begin{bmatrix}
            -\frac{1}{L} & 0            \\
            0            & -\frac{1}{L}
        \end{bmatrix}
        \begin{bmatrix}
            \mathcal{V}^{p}_{\alpha} \\
            \mathcal{V}^{p}_{\beta}
        \end{bmatrix}
    \end{split}
    \label{eq:state_space_alpha_beta_positive}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
        \frac{d}{dt}
        \begin{bmatrix}
            i^{n}_{\alpha} \\
            i^{n}_{\beta}
        \end{bmatrix}
        =
        \begin{bmatrix}
            -\frac{R}{L} & 0            \\
            0            & -\frac{R}{L}
        \end{bmatrix}
        \begin{bmatrix}
            i^{n}_{\alpha} \\
            i^{n}_{\beta}
        \end{bmatrix}
        +
        \begin{bmatrix}
            \frac{1}{L} & 0           \\
            0           & \frac{1}{L}
        \end{bmatrix}
        \begin{bmatrix}
            e^{n}_{\alpha} \\
            e^{n}_{\beta}
        \end{bmatrix}
        +
        \begin{bmatrix}
            -\frac{1}{L} & 0            \\
            0            & -\frac{1}{L}
        \end{bmatrix}
        \begin{bmatrix}
            \mathcal{V}^{n}_{\alpha} \\
            \mathcal{V}^{n}_{\beta}
        \end{bmatrix}
    \end{split}
    \label{eq:state_space_alpha_beta_negative}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{V}^{p}_{\alpha}, \mathcal{V}^{p}_{\beta}$ are the modulation signals for the positive sequence component
and $\mathcal{V}^{n}_{\alpha}, \mathcal{V}^{n}_{\beta}$ are the modulation signals for the negative sequence component.\par
Since both the positive and negative sequence components are decoupled, the system can be transformed into synchronous
rotating frame using the Park transformation as given in appendix [\ref{App:ClarkeParkTransformation}]. The system can be
represented in the synchronous d-q frame as follows:
\begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
        \frac{d}{dt}
        \begin{bmatrix}
            i^{p}_{d} \\
            i^{p}_{q}
        \end{bmatrix}
        &=
        \begin{bmatrix}
            -\frac{R}{L} & \omega       \\
            -\omega      & -\frac{R}{L}
        \end{bmatrix}
        \begin{bmatrix}
            i^{p}_{d} \\
            i^{p}_{q}
        \end{bmatrix}
        +
        \begin{bmatrix}
            \frac{1}{L} & 0           \\
            0           & \frac{1}{L}
        \end{bmatrix}
        \begin{bmatrix}
            e^{p}_{d} \\
            e^{p}_{q}
        \end{bmatrix}
        +
        \begin{bmatrix}
            -\frac{1}{L} & 0            \\
            0            & -\frac{1}{L}
        \end{bmatrix}
        \begin{bmatrix}
            \mathcal{V}^{p}_{d} \\
            \mathcal{V}^{p}_{q}
        \end{bmatrix}
    \end{split}
    \label{eq:state_space_dq_positive}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
        \frac{d}{dt}
        \begin{bmatrix}
            i^{n}_{d} \\
            i^{n}_{q}
        \end{bmatrix}
        &=
        \begin{bmatrix}
            -\frac{R}{L} & -\omega      \\
            \omega       & -\frac{R}{L}
        \end{bmatrix}
        \begin{bmatrix}
            i^{n}_{d} \\
            i^{n}_{q}
        \end{bmatrix}
        +
        \begin{bmatrix}
            \frac{1}{L} & 0           \\
            0           & \frac{1}{L}
        \end{bmatrix}
        \begin{bmatrix}
            e^{n}_{d} \\
            e^{n}_{q}
        \end{bmatrix}
        +
        \begin{bmatrix}
            -\frac{1}{L} & 0            \\
            0            & -\frac{1}{L}
        \end{bmatrix}
        \begin{bmatrix}
            \mathcal{V}^{n}_{d} \\
            \mathcal{V}^{n}_{q}
        \end{bmatrix}
    \end{split}
    \label{eq:state_space_dq_negative}
\end{equation}
The Laplace transformation of equation[\ref{eq:state_space_dq_positive}] and [\ref{eq:state_space_dq_negative}]
gives the transfer function of the system in d-q coordinates and can be shown in figure[\ref{fig:mathematical_model}].
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[scale=0.55]{img/MathematicalModel.png}
    \caption{Mathematical model of VSR in d-q coordinates}
    \label{fig:mathematical_model}
\end{figure}
\section{Effect of unbalanced grid voltage}
The performance of three phase active rectifer is highly infuenced by the grid voltage conditions.
The unbalanced grid voltage can lead to asymmetrical current fow in the system, which can
cause overheating of the components. Also the negative sequence component causes an even
harmonic ripple in the DC link voltage, which can lead to higher harmonic distortion in the
input current.\par
Under unbalanced grid conditions the input apparent power of the system can be expressed as
follows:
\begin{equation}
    S(t) = e_a i_a + e_b i_b + e_c i_c = P(t) + jQ(t)
    \label{eq:apparent_power_equation}
\end{equation}
Applying the Clarke transformation to equation[\ref{eq:apparent_power_equation}] gives the apparent
power in $\alpha\beta$ coordinates as:
\begin{equation}
    S(t) = \frac{3}{2}(e_{\alpha} + je_{\beta})(i_{\alpha} + ji_{\beta})^{*}
    \label{eq:apparent_power_alpha_beta}
\end{equation}
Based on the phasor diagram in figure[\ref{fig:symmetricalDecomposition}], the current vector $\vec{i_{s}}$ can be expressed as vector sum of
$\alpha$ and $\beta$ component currents. Then by inverse Park transformation, $\vec{i_{s}}$ is expressed:
\begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
        \vec{i_{s}} &= i_{\alpha} + ji_{\beta} = i_{\alpha}^{p} + ji_{\beta}^{p} + i_{\alpha}^{n} + ji_{\beta}^{n} \\
        &= (i_{d}^{p} + ji_{q}^{p})e^{j\omega t} + (i_{d}^{n} + ji_{q}^{n})e^{-j\omega t}\\
        &= i_{d}^{p} \cos(\omega t) - i_{q}^{p} \sin(\omega t) + j(i_{d}^{p} \sin(\omega t) + i_{q}^{p} \cos(\omega t))\\
        &+ i_{d}^{n} \cos(\omega t) + i_{q}^{n} \sin(\omega t) + j(-i_{d}^{n} \sin(\omega t) + i_{q}^{n} \cos(\omega t))\\
        &= i_{d}^{p} \cos(\omega t) - i_{q}^{p} \sin(\omega t) + i_{d}^{n} \cos(\omega t) + i_{q}^{n} \sin(\omega t)\\
        &+ j(i_{d}^{p} \sin(\omega t) + i_{q}^{p} \cos(\omega t) - i_{d}^{n} \sin(\omega t) + i_{q}^{n} \cos(\omega t))
    \end{split}
    \label{eq:current_phasor}
\end{equation}
Similarly, the grid voltage signal can be expressed in d-q coordinates as:
\begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
        \vec{e_{s}} &= e_{\alpha} + je_{\beta} = e_{\alpha}^{p} + je_{\beta}^{p} + e_{\alpha}^{n} + je_{\beta}^{n} \\
        &= (e_{d}^{p} + je_{q}^{p})e^{j\omega t} + (e_{d}^{n} + je_{q}^{n})e^{-j\omega t}\\
        &= e_{d}^{p} \cos(\omega t) - e_{q}^{p} \sin(\omega t) + j(e_{d}^{p} \sin(\omega t) + e_{q}^{p} \cos(\omega t))\\
        &+ e_{d}^{n} \cos(\omega t) + e_{q}^{n} \sin(\omega t) + j(-e_{d}^{n} \sin(\omega t) + e_{q}^{n} \cos(\omega t))\\
        &= e_{d}^{p} \cos(\omega t) - e_{q}^{p} \sin(\omega t) + e_{d}^{n} \cos(\omega t) + e_{q}^{n} \sin(\omega t)\\
        &+ j(e_{d}^{p} \sin(\omega t) + e_{q}^{p} cos(\omega t) - e_{d}^{n} \sin(\omega t) + e_{q}^{n} \cos(\omega t))
    \end{split}
    \label{eq:voltage_phasor}
\end{equation}
Substituting the subsequent equations[\ref{eq:current_phasor}] and [\ref{eq:voltage_phasor}] into equation[\ref{eq:apparent_power_alpha_beta}],
the instantaneous active and reactive power can be expressed as:
\begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
        S(t) &= \frac{3}{2}(e_{\alpha} + je_{\beta})(i_{\alpha} + ji_{\beta})^{*} \\
        S(t) &= \frac{3}{2}\Big[ \big(e_{d}^{p}\cos(\omega t) - e_{q}^{p}\sin(\omega t) + e_{d}^{n}\cos(\omega t) + e_{q}^{n}\sin(\omega t)\big) \\
            &\quad + j\big(e_{d}^{p}\sin(\omega t) + e_{q}^{p}\cos(\omega t) - e_{d}^{n}\sin(\omega t) + e_{q}^{n}\cos(\omega t)\big) \Big] \\
        &\quad \times \Big[ \big(i_{d}^{p}\cos(\omega t) - i_{q}^{p}\sin(\omega t) + i_{d}^{n}\cos(\omega t) + i_{q}^{n}\sin(\omega t)\big) \\
            &\quad - j\big(i_{d}^{p}\sin(\omega t) + i_{q}^{p}\cos(\omega t) - i_{d}^{n}\sin(\omega t) + i_{q}^{n}\cos(\omega t)\big)\Big]
    \end{split}
    \label{eq:apparent_power_dq}
\end{equation}
The simplified form of equation[\ref{eq:apparent_power_dq}] gives the instantaneous active power $P(t)$ and reactive power $Q(t)$ as follows:
\begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
        P(t) &= P_0 + P_{c2}\cos(2\omega t) + P_{s2}\sin(2\omega t) \\
        Q(t) &= Q_0 + Q_{c2}\cos(2\omega t) + Q_{s2}\sin(2\omega t)
    \end{split}
    \label{eq:instantaneous_power}
\end{equation}
where $P_0$ and $Q_0$ are the average active and reactive power components, $P_{c2}, P_{s2}, Q_{c2}, Q_{s2}$ are the coefficients of the
second harmonic components of active and reactive power respectively are caused by the voltage unbalance.The real power delivered to the
DC link and determines the dc voltage level. Hence, if $P(t)$ varies with
time for $P_{c2}, P_{s2}$ not being equal to zero, the DC-link voltage fluctuates, i.e, $2\omega$ ripple appears. Therefore, to keep constant
DC level, $P_{c2}, P_{s2}$ have to be nullified. Also the dc component reactive power $Q_0$ should be nullified to achieve unity power
factor in average.\par
\begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
        P_0 &= \frac{3}{2}(e_{d}^{p} i_{d}^{p} + e_{q}^{p} i_{q}^{p} + e_{d}^{n} i_{d}^{n} + e_{q}^{n} i_{q}^{n}) \\
        P_{c2} &= \frac{3}{2}(e_{d}^{p} i_{d}^{n} + e_{q}^{p} i_{q}^{n} + e_{d}^{n} i_{d}^{p} + e_{q}^{n} i_{q}^{p}) \\
        P_{s2} &= \frac{3}{2}(e_{q}^{n} i_{d}^{p} - e_{d}^{n} i_{q}^{p} - e_{q}^{p} i_{d}^{n} + e_{d}^{p} i_{q}^{n}) \\
        Q_0 &= \frac{3}{2}(e_{q}^{p} i_{d}^{p} - e_{d}^{p} i_{q}^{p} + e_{q}^{n} i_{d}^{n} - e_{d}^{n} i_{q}^{n}) \\
        Q_{c2} &= \frac{3}{2}(e_{q}^{p} i_{d}^{n} - e_{d}^{p} i_{q}^{n} + e_{q}^{n} i_{d}^{p} - e_{d}^{n} i_{q}^{p}) \\
        Q_{s2} &= \frac{3}{2}(e_{d}^{p} i_{d}^{n} + e_{q}^{p} i_{q}^{n} - e_{d}^{n} i_{d}^{p} - e_{q}^{n} i_{q}^{p})
    \end{split}
    \label{eq:power_coefficients}
\end{equation}
\section{DC link model}
The DC link voltage dynamics can be modeled as a first-order system, where the input is the power
injected into the DC link and the output is the DC link voltage. The energy stored in the DC link capacitor is given by:\par
\begin{equation}
    E_{c} = \frac{1}{2} C V_{dc}^{2}
    \label{eq:dc_link_energy}
\end{equation}
where $C$ is the capacitance of the DC link capacitor and $V_{dc}$ is the DC link voltage.\par
The power balance equation for the DC link can be expressed as:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{1}{2}C \frac{dV^{2}_{dc}}{dt} = P_{in} - P_{load}
    \label{eq:dc_link_power_balance}
\end{equation}
where $P_{in}$ is the power injected into the DC link from the AC side and $P_{load}$ is the power drawn from the DC link to the load or grid.
We can choose a new state variable $W = V_{dc}^{2}$, then the DC link voltage dynamics can be represented as:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{1}{2}C \frac{dW}{dt} = P_{in} - P_{load}
    \label{eq:dc_link_dynamics}
\end{equation}
\section{Space vector modulation}
The control strategies discussed in Chapter 3 generate reference voltage vectors necessary to regulate the grid currents
and DC link voltage. To synthesize these reference voltages across the terminals of the three-phase rectifier, a modulation
stage is required to translate continuous voltage commands into discrete switching states for the IGBTs.
Space Vector Modulation (SVM) is selected for this work due to its superior utilization of the DC-link voltage
($15\%$ higher than sinusoidal PWM) and lower total harmonic distortion (THD).\par
In SVM, the three-phase voltages are represented as a space vector in the complex plane. The switching states
of the inverter are determined based on the position of the reference voltage vector and the available switching
states.\par
\subsection{Voltage Space Vectors}
As defined in the system model in equation[\ref{eq:common_mode_voltage}], the rectifier terminal voltages $\mathcal{V}_{a,b,c}$
are determined by the switching states $S_{a,b,c}$ and the DC-link voltage $V_{dc}$. In a three-phase two-level converter,
there are $2^3 = 8$ possible switching states. These states can be mapped to stationary reference frame vectors using the Clarke Transformation.
\begin{equation}
    \vec{V} = \frac{2}{3} V_{dc} (S_a + S_b e^{j2\pi/3} + S_c e^{-j2\pi/3})
    \label{eq:volage_vector}
\end{equation}
This transformation results in:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Six Active Vectors ($\vec{V}_1$ to $\vec{V}_6$): These form a regular hexagon in the $\alpha\beta$ plane,
          dividing it into six sectors (I to VI). They have a magnitude of $\frac{2}{3}V_{dc}$.
    \item Two Zero Vectors ($\vec{V}_0$ and $\vec{V}_7$): Located at the origin. $\vec{V}_0$ corresponds to state [000]
          and $\vec{V}_7$ to [111]. These vectors apply zero voltage to the load (shorting the terminals).
\end{itemize}
\subsection{Reference Vector Synthesis}
The current controllers output a reference voltage vector $\vec{V}_{ref}$ in the $dq$ frame.
For modulation, this is transformed into the stationary $\alpha\beta$ frame:
\begin{equation}
    \vec{V}_{ref} = (V_{\alpha}^{p*} + jV_{\beta}^{p*})+(V_{\alpha}^{n*} + jV_{\beta}^{n*})=V_{\alpha}^* + jV_{\beta}^* = |\vec{V}_{ref}| \angle \theta_{ref}
    \label{eq:reference_vector}
\end{equation}
The objective of SVM is to approximate $\vec{V}_{ref}$ by averaging the two adjacent active vectors ($\vec{V}_x, \vec{V}_y$)
and the zero vectors over a switching period $T_s$. The principle of volt-second balance is applied:
\begin{equation}
    \int_{0}^{T_s} \vec{V}_{ref} dt = \int_{0}^{T_1} \vec{V}_x dt + \int_{0}^{T_2} \vec{V}_y dt + \int_{0}^{T_0} \vec{V}_z dt
    \label{eq:volt_second_balance}
\end{equation}
Assuming constant $\vec{V}_{ref}$ over the small period $T_s$:
\begin{equation}
    \vec{V}_{ref} \cdot T_s = \vec{V}_x \cdot T_1 + \vec{V}_y \cdot T_2 + \vec{V}_z \cdot T_0
\end{equation}
Where $T_1$ and $T_2$ are the dwell times for the active vectors, and $T_0$ is the total dwell time for the zero vectors.
\subsection{Dwell Time Calculation}
For a reference vector located in Sector I (bounded by vectors $\vec{V}_1 [100]$ and $\vec{V}_2 [110]$),
the dwell times can be derived by projecting the vectors onto the $\alpha$ and $\beta$ axes:
\begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
        T_1 &= \frac{\sqrt{3} \cdot T_s \cdot |\vec{V}_{ref}|}{V_{dc}} \sin\left(\frac{\pi}{3} - \theta\right)\\
        T_2 &= \frac{\sqrt{3} \cdot T_s \cdot |\vec{V}_{ref}|}{V_{dc}} \sin(\theta)\\
        T_0 &= T_s - T_1 - T_2\\
    \end{split}
\end{equation}
where $\theta$ is the angle of $\vec{V}_{ref}$ relative to the start of the sector ($0 \le \theta \le 60^\circ$).\par
This calculation generalizes to any sector $n$ by substituting $\theta$ with $\theta' = \theta - (n-1)\frac{\pi}{3}$.\par
\subsection{Switching Sequence}
To minimize switching losses, the transition from one state to the next is performed by changing the state of only one leg at a time.
A symmetrical switching sequence is typically employed. For Sector I, the sequence is:
\begin{equation}
    \vec{V}_0 \rightarrow \vec{V}_1 \rightarrow \vec{V}_2 \rightarrow \vec{V}_7 \rightarrow \vec{V}_7 \rightarrow \vec{V}_2 \rightarrow \vec{V}_1 \rightarrow \vec{V}_0
\end{equation}
This results in the gate signals having a center-aligned pulse pattern, which reduces the harmonic content of the
output current compared to asymmetrical patterns. The modulation index $m_a$ is defined as:
\begin{equation}
    m_a = \frac{\sqrt{3} |\vec{V}_{ref}|}{V_{dc}}
\end{equation}
The linear modulation range is limited to $m_a \le 1$, which corresponds to the reference vector remaining within
the inscribed circle of the hexagon ($\frac{V_{dc}}{\sqrt{3}}$).
