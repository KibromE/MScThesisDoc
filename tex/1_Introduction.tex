\chapter{Introduction}
\section{Background}
Power electronic converters are an electrical circuit that convert electrical energy
from on level of voltage/current/frequency to another using a semiconductor based 
electronic switches and energy storage components \cite{holmes2003pulse,mohan2003power,
rodriguez2012predictive}. As the demand for expansion of renewable energy and high efficiency
energy conversion is growing, power electronic systems are widely being used to process
the available power to meet load requirements. For this reason, a Three-Phase AC/DC active
rectifier has been gaining momentum in high power electric vehicles (EV) charging stations,
renewable energy generation, industrial motor control and power supplies.\par

Traditionally, rectifiers based on diodes and thyristors are able to provide uncontrolled
and controlled DC Output respectively. But both converters have low power factor, 
non-sinusoidal input current, high input harmonic distortion and low-frequency voltage 
ripple in the output side which requirements large filters in both AC and DC sides, However
those filters are very costly and bulky. With the development self-commutating power electronic
devices (MOSFET, IGBT \& GTO etc.) many topologies have developed to overcome the shortcomings 
of the conventional converters, such as multilevel, multipulse, boost, buck, PFC etc.
\cite{articleKolarI,articleKolarII,ReviewSafayatullah,batteries9030150}. Among the
improved power converters, a Pulse Width Modulation (PWM) based power converters preferred for 
their high efficiency.\par

The three phase two level boost rectifier shown in fig.\ref{fig:topology} is a PWM controlled,
low-cost, well-known and robust topology consisting of six active switches, a DC link capacitor and 
three AC side inductors with continuous input current, bidirectional power flow capability and 
nearly unity power factor performance. Since the output of the rectifier is connected to DC 
capacitor, which often supplies a controlled DC voltage, it is called a \textit{voltage source rectifier} (VSR).

\section{Literature review}

\subsection{Introduction to Active Voltage Source Rectifiers}
To establish the hardware context of the thesis, it is essential to understand the topologies available 
for three-phase power factor correction (PFC). The article \cite{friedli2014} provide a comprehensive
comparative evaluation of active rectifiers, categorizing them into boost-type and buck-type systems. 
They highlight that boost-type rectifiers, such as the six-switch boost-type and the VIENNA rectifier, 
are widely used for industrial applications requiring controlled DC output voltage and sinusoidal input currents. 
For applications requiring high power density and efficiency, the VIENNA rectifier is noted for its three-level 
characteristic, which reduces inductor volume compared to two-level topologies. However, the active six-switch 
boost-type rectifier remains a robust standard for bidirectional power flow capabilities \cite{friedli2014}.

\subsection{Mathematical Modeling of Unbalanced Grid Conditions}
Understanding the mathematical decomposition of grid faults is crucial for controlling output voltage under 
unbalanced conditions. \cite{paap2000} details the application of symmetrical components in the time domain, 
a fundamental theory for analyzing asymmetries. The transformation of arbitrary instantaneous variables into 
positive, negative, and zero-sequence components allows for the decoupling of control systems. This paper emphasizes 
that under unbalanced conditions, the negative-sequence component is the complex conjugate of the positive sequence
in the time domain, which is vital for calculating instantaneous power and detecting faults \cite{paap2000}.

\subsection{Traditional Control Strategies for Unbalanced Grid Voltage}
Before introducing Model Predictive Control (MPC), it is necessary to review traditional linear control methods 
used to mitigate unbalance.The Paper \cite{song1999} addressed the issue where voltage unbalance causes 120-Hz 
ripples in the DC-link voltage and increases reactive power. They proposed a dual current control scheme using two 
synchronous reference frames (SRF): one rotating counter-clockwise for the positive sequence and one clockwise for 
the negative sequence. By filtering out the oscillating components in each frame, they achieved constant DC-link 
voltage regulation without increasing control gains to dangerous levels \cite{song1999}.\par
While effective, Proportional-Integral (PI) controllers face stability issues under dynamic fault conditions. 
The article \cite{mirhosseini2013} discuss the stability restrictions of PI parameters when using Moving 
Average Filters (MAF) to extract sequence components. The delay introduced by filtering techniques slows down the 
dynamic response of the system, creating a tradeoff between stability regions and fast dynamic performance. 
They suggest that alternative controllers, such as Proportional-Resonant (PR) or MPC, may offer faster dynamics 
\cite{mirhosseini2013}.\par
The article \cite{camacho2016} explored strategies to maximize voltage support during grid faults. They developed 
optimal solutions to maximize positive sequence voltage or minimize negative sequence voltage while injecting maximum
rated current. Their work highlights that optimal power reference generation depends heavily on the grid impedance 
($L/R$ ratio) \cite{camacho2016}. Similarly, The article \cite{alathamneh2022} proposed a method using time-domain
symmetrical component extraction to balance grid currents and eliminate power oscillations, thereby stabilizing the DC-link voltage.\par

\subsection{Model Predictive Control of Active Voltage Source Rectifiers}
Model Predictive Control (MPC) has emerged as a powerful control strategy for power electronic converters,
offering advantages such as constraint handling and multivariable control. \cite{rodriguez2012predictive}
provide a comprehensive overview of MPC applications in power electronics, highlighting its ability to predict future system behavior
and optimize control actions accordingly. They emphasize that MPC can effectively manage the nonlinearities and constraints inherent 
in power electronic systems.\par
Specifically for active voltage source rectifiers, MPC has been shown to improve performance under unbalanced grid conditions. 
The paper \cite{wang2015pid} developed an MPC strategy that directly controls the output voltage of a three-phase VSR under 
unbalanced conditions. Their approach involves predicting the future states of the system and optimizing the switching actions
to minimize voltage deviations and harmonic distortion \cite{wang2015pid}.\par
Furthermore, The article \cite{jin2018} proposed an MPC with direct power control scheme that incorporates a cost function designed to minimize
both the output voltage error and the switching losses. Their results demonstrate that MPC can achieve superior voltage regulation
and reduced harmonic content compared to traditional PI controllers, particularly during unbalanced grid events \cite{jin2018}.\par
The literature indicates that while traditional dual-loop PI control (as described in \cite{song1999}) can handle unbalanced
grid conditions, it suffers from bandwidth limitations and filtering delays \cite{mirhosseini2013}.
The proposed thesis topic, applying MPC to this problem, is supported by the work of \cite{kouro2009} and  \cite{vazquez2014},
which demonstrates MPC's superior dynamic performance and ability to handle multivariable constraints. By integrating the 
symmetrical component theories in \cite{paap2000} and the voltage support strategies of \cite{camacho2016} into an MPC framework,
the thesis can address the DC-link voltage ripple and power oscillation problems more effectively than legacy methods.
Overall, the literature indicates that MPC offers significant advantages for controlling active voltage source rectifiers, 
especially under challenging grid conditions. Its predictive capabilities and optimization framework make it a promising 
approach for enhancing the performance and reliability of power electronic converters.
