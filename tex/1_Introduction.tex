\chapter{Introduction}
\section{Background}
Power electronic converters are an electrical circuit that convert electrical energy
from on level of voltage/current/frequency to another using a semiconductor based 
electronic switches and energy storage components \cite{holmes2003pulse,mohan2003power,
rodriguez2012predictive}. As the demand for expansion of renewable energy and high efficiency
energy conversion is growing, power electronic systems are widely being used to process
the available power to meet load requirements. For this reason, a Three-Phase AC/DC active
rectifier has been gaining momentum in high power electric vehicles (EV) charging stations,
renewable energy generation, industrial motor control and power supplies.\par
Traditionally, rectifiers based on diodes and thyristors are able to provide uncontrolled
and controlled DC Output respectively. But both converters have low power factor, 
non-sinusoidal input current, high input harmonic distortion and low-frequency voltage 
ripple in the output side which requirements large filters in both AC and DC sides, However
those filters are very costly and bulky. With the development self-commutating power electronic
devices (MOSFET, IGBT and GTO etc.) many topologies have developed to overcome the shortcomings 
of the conventional converters, such as multilevel, multipulse, boost, buck, PFC etc.
\cite{articleKolarI,articleKolarII,ReviewSafayatullah,batteries9030150}. Among the
improved power converters, a Pulse Width Modulation (PWM) based power converters preferred for 
their high efficiency.\par
The three phase two level boost rectifier shown in Figure \ref{fig:topology} is a PWM controlled,
low-cost, well-known and robust topology consisting of six active switches, a DC link capacitor and 
three AC side inductors with continuous input current, bidirectional power flow capability and 
nearly unity power factor performance. Since the output of the rectifier has a stiff DC voltage source
characteristic, it is called a voltage source rectifier(VSR).

\section{Literature review}
\subsection{Introduction to Active Voltage Source Rectifiers}
To establish the hardware context of the thesis, it is essential to understand the topologies available 
for three-phase active voltage source rectifiers. The literature in \cite{friedli2014} provide a comprehensive
comparative evaluation of active rectifiers, categorizing them into boost-type and buck-type systems. 
They highlight that boost-type rectifiers, such as the six-switch boost-type and the VIENNA rectifier, 
are widely used for industrial applications requiring controlled DC output voltage and sinusoidal input currents. 
For applications requiring high power density and efficiency, the VIENNA rectifier is noted for its three-level 
characteristic, which reduces inductor volume compared to two-level topologies. However, the active six-switch 
boost-type rectifier remains a robust standard for bidirectional power flow capabilities \cite{friedli2014}.\par
\subsection{Classical Control Strategies for Unbalanced Grid Voltage}
Before introducing Model Predictive Control (MPC), it is necessary to review traditional linear control methods 
used to mitigate unbalance. The research in \cite{song1999} addresses the issue where voltage unbalance causes $2\omega$ 
ripples in the DC-link voltage and increases reactive power. They proposed a dual current control scheme using two 
synchronous reference frames (SRF): one rotating counter-clockwise for the positive sequence and one clockwise for 
the negative sequence. By filtering out the oscillating components in each frame, they achieved constant DC-link 
voltage regulation without increasing control gains to dangerous levels \cite{song1999}.\par
Though Proportional-Integral (PI) controllers are simple, effective, and linear, but they face stability issues
under dynamic fault conditions. The study in \cite{mirhosseini2013} discuss the stability restrictions of PI 
parameters when using Moving Average Filters (MAF) to extract sequence components. The delay introduced by
filtering techniques slows down the dynamic response of the system, creating a tradeoff between stability regions
and fast dynamic performance. They suggest that alternative controllers, such as Proportional-Resonant (PR) or MPC,
may offer faster dynamics \cite{mirhosseini2013}.\par
The research conducted by Camacho et al. in \cite{camacho2016} explores strategies to maximize voltage support during 
grid faults. They developed optimal solutions to maximize positive sequence voltage or minimize negative sequence
voltage while injecting maximum rated current. Their work highlights that optimal power reference generation depends
heavily on the grid impedance ($L/R$ ratio) \cite{camacho2016}. Similarly, The article \cite{alathamneh2022} proposed
a method using time-domain symmetrical component extraction to balance grid currents and eliminate power oscillations,
thereby stabilizing the DC-link voltage.\par
\subsection{Model Predictive Control of Active Voltage Source Rectifiers}
Model Predictive Control (MPC) has emerged as a powerful control strategy for power electronic converters,
offering advantages such as constraint handling and multivariable control. \cite{rodriguez2012predictive}
provide a comprehensive overview of MPC applications in power electronics, highlighting its ability to predict
future system behavior and optimize control actions accordingly. They emphasize that MPC can effectively manage
the nonlinearities and constraints inherent in power electronic systems.\par
Specifically for active voltage source rectifiers, MPC has been shown to improve performance under unbalanced grid conditions. 
Wang et al. \cite{wang2015pid} explores an MPC strategy that directly controls the output voltage of a three-phase VSR under 
unbalanced conditions. Their approach involves predicting the future states of the system and optimizing the switching actions
to minimize voltage deviations and harmonic distortion \cite{wang2015pid}. The study conducted in \cite{jin2018} proposed
an MPC with direct power control scheme that incorporates a cost function designed to minimize both the output voltage error
and the switching losses. Their results demonstrate that MPC can achieve superior voltage regulation and reduced harmonic
content compared to classical PI controllers, particularly during unbalanced grid events \cite{jin2018}; while both suffer
variable switching frequency.\par
As indicated above, the extensive research on classical dual-loop controllers and advanced predictive techniques, a 
significant gap remains in efficiently mitigating severe grid unbalances without compromising dynamic response and
maintain constant switch frequency. classical linear control schemes, such as dual current control using
PI controllers \cite{song1999}, heavily rely on filtering techniques (e.g.,notch or Moving Average Filters)
to extract sequence components. These filters inherently introduce delays that slow down the dynamic response \cite{mirhosseini2013}.
While Model Predictive Control (MPC) is proven to offer superior dynamic performance and the ability to seamlessly handle multivariable
constraints, its specific application to mitigating DC-link voltage ripples through a discrete-time prediction model based on
Symmetrical Sequence Decomposition remains insufficiently explored. Therefore, there is a clear need for a robust control architecture
that leverages MPC to directly optimize both positive and negative sequence components, thereby eliminating the sluggishness of
classical filters while maintaining highly regulated DC output and unity power factor during asymmetrical grid faults.\par
\section{Statement of the problem}
Under practical operating conditions, power grids frequently experience asymmetrical faults that severely degrade the performance
and reliability of three-phase Active Voltage Source Rectifiers (VSRs). The negative sequence components causes an even harmonic 
ripple at twice the fundamental grid frequency in the DC link voltage. This continuous fluctuation prevents a stiff 
DC output and directly leads to higher harmonic distortion in the input AC currents. Because classical linear PI controllers suffer
from bandwidth limitations, the fundamental problem addressed in this research is how to design a robust predictive control framework
capable of decoupling these sequence components, proactively suppressing the second-order power oscillations, and maintaining strict
DC-link voltage regulation during severe grid unbalances.\par
\section{Objective}
\subsection{General Objective}
The main objective of this thesis is to design a Model Predictive Control (MPC) strategy for a three-phase active VSR by controlling
separately the positive and negative sequence components in to achieve regulated output voltage and suppress harmonic ripples under
unbalanced grid voltage conditions. 
\subsection{Specific Objectives}
The specific objectives to achieve the general objective of this thesis are to:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Adopt a mathematical model for the three phase active voltage source rectifier.
    \item Design an internal PI current controller.
    \item Develop a discretized Prediction Model.
    \item Design of cost function structure and select weighting factors.
    \item Simulate the control system and VSR under different grid conditions.
\end{itemize}
\section{Thesis organization}
This thesis is organized into five chapters, structured to provide a comprehensive analysis of the proposed control strategy for
a three-phase active voltage source rectifier under unbalanced grid conditions. The content of each chapter is outlined as follows.\par
The second chapter develops the comprehensive mathematical model of the three-phase Voltage Source Rectifier (VSR). It details the
derivation of the voltage and current equations in the synchronous reference frame and introduces the method of Symmetrical Sequence
Decomposition to analyze positive and negative sequence components. Furthermore, it analyzes the power flow equations to mathematically
demonstrate how unbalanced grid voltages introduce second-order harmonic ripples in the DC-link voltage, which serves as the foundation
for the control problem. Also describe the formulation and generation of space vector modulation PWM.\par
Chapter three presents the core contribution of the thesis: the design of the proposed control architecture. It details the cascaded
control structure, consisting of an inner-loop PI current controller for decoupling and regulating AC currents, and an outer-loop
Model Predictive Controller (MPC) for DC-link voltage regulation. The chapter provides a step-by-step formulation of the MPC prediction model,
the design of the cost function for ripple suppression, the handling of constraints, and the selection of the optimization solver.
Additionally, the design of the Phase Locked Loop (PLL) for accurate synchronization under distorted grid conditions is described.\par
The fourth chapter validates the proposed control strategy through extensive simulations using MATLAB/Simulink. It outlines the system 
and controller parameters and defines the specific test scenarios, including balanced grid conditions and various unbalanced fault types.
The results are analyzed to evaluate the controller's ability to maintain DC voltage regulation, suppress harmonic ripples, and
ensure unity power factor.\par
The final chapter summarizes the key findings and contributions of this research. It draws conclusions regarding the effectiveness of the
proposed MPC strategy in mitigating the adverse effects of grid unbalance. Finally, it suggests potential areas for future research and
improvements to further enhance the performance and applicability of the control system.\par
